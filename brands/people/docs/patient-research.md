## Primary Patient Avatar: "The Frustrated Active Adult"

**Demographics**
- Ages 30–55
- Physically active or formerly active
- College educated, professionally employed
- Family oriented — motivated by showing up for their kids, partners, and community
- Has disposable income and is willing to invest in their health when they trust the provider
- Located in a community with access to gyms, CrossFit, golf, recreational sports, or outdoor activity

**Their Story**  
They were once active and athletic, or they currently are — but something is getting in the way. Maybe it's a nagging injury that never fully healed, postpartum changes, chronic pain they've just "learned to live with," or a performance plateau they can't break through. They've seen other providers and left feeling unheard, rushed, or handed a generic plan that didn't stick.

**Pain Points**
- Pain or limitations that won't go away no matter what they try
- Short, impersonal visits where they felt like just another number
- Being told to "rest" or "just get adjusted" without any real explanation
- Fear that they'll never get back to doing what they love
- Feeling like their body is working against them

**What They Believe**
- "I've tried chiropractic before and it didn't really work long term"
- "I just have to manage this — it's probably not going to get better"
- "I don't want surgery or medication — there has to be another way"
- "I want to understand what's actually wrong with me"
- "I want someone who sees me as an individual, not a diagnosis"

**What Keeps Them Up at Night**
- Will I be able to keep up with my kids as they get older?
- Am I going to have to give up the sport or activity I love?
- Is this just what getting older feels like?
- Why haven't any of my previous providers actually fixed this?
- Am I going to end up needing surgery?

**What They're Looking For**  
A provider who actually listens, takes the time to understand the full picture, explains the "why" behind their pain, and gives them a real plan — not just temporary relief. They want to feel empowered, not dependent. They want a partner in their health, not just someone to crack their back and send them home.

## Three Patient Prototypes

### 🔄 Recover — "Reconnect"
**Core desire:** Get back to doing what they love

This patient was once active and engaged in something they loved, but life got in the way — through childbirth, surgery, an old injury, or chronic pain. Their core desire is to get back to the activities and identity they've lost. They may carry frustration, grief, or fear around movement, and often feel like their body has failed them.

**Common triggers:** Postpartum changes, post-surgical recovery, old injuries that stopped activity, chronic pain  
**What they need:** A provider who can help them rebuild trust in their body and create a clear path back to what matters most to them.

**Content angles that work:**
- "It's not too late to get back"
- "Your body hasn't failed you — it needs the right support"
- "What recovery actually looks like with a real plan"
- "Why 'just rest it' isn't the answer"

---

### ⚡ Longevity — "Continue"
**Core desire:** Keep doing what they love as they age

This patient is currently active and wants to stay that way. They're not chasing a new peak — they want to protect what they have. They're proactive about their health, understand the value of investing in their body now, and are motivated by the idea of compounding small gains over time. Their fear is losing the ability to do what they love as they age.

**Common triggers:** Aging concerns, wanting to stay ahead of injury, fear of losing capability  
**What they need:** A long-term health partner, not just a fix for a single problem.

**Content angles that work:**
- "Small investments now that pay off in decades"
- "How to train smarter, not just harder"
- "What proactive care actually looks like"
- "Staying active into your 50s, 60s, and beyond"

---

### 🎯 Performance — "Excel"
**Core desire:** Perform at a higher level without breaking down

This patient has a specific goal they're working toward — a competition, a personal record, or simply being the best version of themselves in their sport or activity. They're driven, results-oriented, and respond well to data and measurable progress. Whether a dedicated athlete or a weekend warrior, they see their body as a tool to be optimized.

**Common triggers:** Performance plateau, training for a competition, optimizing athletic output  
**What they need:** A provider who takes their goals seriously and can help them perform at a higher level.

**Content angles that work:**
- "What separates athletes who improve from those who plateau"
- "How to optimize your body for your sport"
- "Understanding the mechanics behind your performance"
- "Training without accumulating damage"

## Common Pain Points with Prior Providers

These are frustrations patients bring in from prior chiropractic, PT, or medical care. Use them to shape content that differentiates Move Better without directly attacking competitors.

**Chiropractic frustrations**
- Coming in for treatment 3× a week or long-term weekly without a clear end in sight
- Lack of clarity around treatment plan or what realistic results to expect
- Short, rushed visits that feel impersonal
- Maintenance care without meaningful progress or explanation
- Unexpected pain or worsening symptoms following care with no communication
- Perceived over-treatment — too many visits without justification
- Unwanted post-treatment symptoms that weren't warned about

**Physical therapy frustrations**
- One-size-fits-all exercise programs that are generic or repetitive
- Little individualization or meaningful progression in exercises
- Not perceiving significant improvement over time; goals aren't clearly set or tracked
- Seeing multiple patients at once, being delegated to aides, feeling rushed
- Concerns not being heard or addressed during visits
- Not enough explanation about the condition or why certain exercises matter

### Dr. Q
**Ideal patient:** Intellectually curious, self-aware individuals who think they want passive care but can be guided toward active engagement. Someone with a personal story around aging well, flexible in their thinking, passionate about something they pursue. May include younger patients just beginning to explore exercise.

**Raw notes:** Someone that has a story behind how they want to age well, didn't vote for trump, flexible intelligence, spends time on a thing they're passionate about, high school age starting to first explore exercise, likes working with people who think they just want passive care and showing them they really want what we do — active care. Dr. Q feels in order to do this you need to be good at mentally disarming people and getting them out of their own way.

**Clinical specialty:** Intellectually disarming patients; bridging passive-care expectations to active engagement

### Tyler

**Raw notes:** Positive relationship with movement, clear goal in mind, enjoy the challenges of movement/new movement.

### Zach
**Ideal patient:** Combat athletes (fighters, grapplers), overweight individuals, and people with secondary goals of body composition improvement, metabolic health, and staying injury-free while continuing to train and compete.

**Conditions he enjoys treating:** Neck pain, neck/scapula shoulder dysfunction, MCL and ACL injuries, chronic low back pain, weight-related pain.

**Common patient pain points:** Poor relationship with the gym, fear of working out or intimidated by the gym, emotional component with exercise.

**Raw notes:** Fighters, grapplers, combat athletes, overweight individuals, people with a secondary goal of body composition, improvements in metabolic health, continue training without injuries, having success with competition, losing weight, improving athletic performance, and improving relationship with their body. Common pain points: poor relationship with gym, fear of working out or intimidated by the gym, emotional component with exercise.

**Conditions she enjoys treating:** Shoulder and knee cases, chronic injury, multiple aches and pains.

**Notes on patient connection:** Connects especially well with middle-aged dads who have softened and want to get better for their kids — patients who recognize they're not as athletic as they once were and are ready to listen.

**Raw notes:** Someone once athletic in youth and had poor experience or injury and fear avoidance, especially when they have a want to get back into it. Usually have a lot of aches and pains, or chronic injury (she specifically likes to work with shoulder and knee cases), they live in mindset of "it's broken and is what it is." Middle-aged dads that have a softness and want to listen and get better for their kids, realize they're not as athletic as they used to be. Also enjoys working with Powerlifters as that is a passion of Sophie's.

### Hope
**Ideal patient:** Older adults who have been dismissed or underestimated due to age, and pregnant individuals who've been treated as overly fragile.

**What motivates her:** Surprised by how robust and resilient older patients can be. Loves rebuilding mind-muscle connection and body awareness.

**Common patient pain points (older adult):** Old age is usually thought of as frail and afraid of movement, being incapable. Often dismissed based on age. Poor proprioception and body awareness.

**Common patient pain points (pregnant):** Often treated like they are super fragile. Wants to give space for these patients to feel seen, heard, and taken care of.

**Raw notes:** Working with the older population, she is surprised by how robust and resilient they can be. Common pain point: old age is usually thought of as frail and afraid of movement, being incapable. Often dismissed based on age. She wants to make this population feel heard and seen and given the time of day; she likes helping rebuild mind/muscle correction. Common pain point: does not have good proprioception; she likes helping them find that body awareness. She also likes serving the pregnant population, fascinated by changes in body. Pain point: often treated like they are super fragile. Hope likes to give space for these patients to feel seen, heard and taken care of.

**Conditions/focus areas:** Breathing dysfunction, overtraining, load management for young athletes.

**Personal perspective:** His own philosophy has matured — rather than being fastest or strongest, he wants to be healthy and feel good, able to move well through life, making small changes today that compound through life.

**Common patient pain points:** Over-training in young athletes.

**Raw notes:** A want to improve, not breathing right, perspective shift as he has matured — rather than to be the fastest or strongest he wants to be healthy and feel good in his body and able to move well through life. Making small changes today that can compound through life. Likes working with young athletes: football and track specifically. Pain points: over-training young athletes.
